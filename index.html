Paper · 2025

Determinants of the Early-Exercise Premium for American Puts

Built a reusable framework to infer the Early-Exercise Premium (EEP), the additional value of an American put over its European counterpart, directly from American option chains without requiring European comparables. The core idea: bound EEP between no-arbitrage limits derived from put-call parity, then use Monte Carlo simulation (1,000 runs, ~486k simulated points with antithetic variates) to sample the latent premium within those bounds. Bounds calculation was fully automated for reproducibility and scalability, and GPU acceleration (CuPy) reduced runtime 26× (from 5.6s to 0.2s) on workloads equivalent to 25M option pricing rows.

Applied to a single SPY $560 put chain (Bloomberg Terminal data) and confirmed with >99.99% confidence that EEP declines as expiration approaches and rises with implied volatility — consistent with theory. The specific regression results are not meant to generalize beyond this chain; the contribution is the framework itself, which can be reapplied to other underlyings, strikes, and time periods.

Competition · 2025

College Federal Reserve Challenge — Team Analyst

Competed as part of a USC team in the College Fed Challenge, a national competition judged by Federal Reserve economists where teams simulate an FOMC meeting and defend a rate decision. Our October 2025 presentation, "Navigating Monetary Policy with No Risk-Free Path," assessed four areas of the economy: growth, inflation, the labor market, and financial conditions.

The core tension we identified: inflation was still running well above target (Core CPI at 2.9%, Core PCE at ~3.1%, PPI at 4.6%), with 1-year inflation expectations rising back to 3.4% — all pointing to unfinished business on price stability. The labor market, while soft in spots (June 2025 marked the first net job loss since COVID), was broadly balanced: the unemployment-to-job-openings ratio had returned to 1:1, and the Sahm Rule indicator had declined to 0.24%, suggesting gradual cooling rather than a recessionary break.

Our recommendation at the October 2025 meeting was to hold the Fed Funds Rate at 4.0–4.25%. We argued that further cuts would exacerbate still-elevated inflation without providing meaningful relief to a labor market that was softening gradually, not collapsing. We also flagged that markets were pricing in 50 bps of additional cuts by year-end, making a hold a credibility-reinforcing signal on the Fed's inflation commitment.

Featured · 2025

The Body's Response to Chronic Anxiety and Stress

Ink, coffee wash, acrylic marker on paper

Most illustrations of anxiety focus on the psychological or social dimensions: what it feels like, or how it's perceived. This piece tries to fill a different gap, asking what is actually happening inside the body. It draws on the biology of chronic stress (the HPA axis, allostatic load, and the SAM system) and looks at how medications like Escitalopram partially, but not completely, restore order.

…more

Allostatic Load. The vines wrapping around the brain and chest represent neural atrophy and declining muscle protein, both hallmarks of prolonged HPA axis activation. The coffee stains on the canvas stand in for bone deterioration and the late nights caused by stress-related sleep disturbances. The chaotic scribbles are the feedback loop itself: stress triggers cortisol release, resistance builds, which demands even more cortisol, which compounds the damage.

Sympatho-Adrenomedullary System. The heart sitting outside the ribcage represents the physical sensation of a panic attack, specifically the feeling of your heart beating out of your chest. Vines around the throat simulate the shortness of breath that accompanies acute anxiety episodes.

Mitigants. The magenta boxes are the only structured, geometric elements in the piece, representing SSRIs like Escitalopram. They bring order to isolated corners of the composition. But the surrounding chaos remains: medication reduces cortisol, not everything.
